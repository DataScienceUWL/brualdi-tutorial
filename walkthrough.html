§1. Introduction

The objects: matrices, interchanges, and the interchange graph

Definition. A 2×2 interchange replaces a [[1,0],[0,1]] submatrix by [[0,1],[1,0]] (or vice versa), leaving all other entries fixed. The interchange graph G(R,S) has the matrices of 𝒜(R,S) as vertices, two adjacent when one interchange turns one into the other.

Worked example (E1) — the smallest non-bipartite class. R=(2,1), S=(1,1,1): 2×3 matrices, row sums 2,1, every column sum 1. The matrix is pinned down by which column holds row 1’s single 1, so there are exactly three, and all three are pairwise one interchange apart — G is the triangle K₃. Verified: |V|=3, connected, 1 triangle.

Ryser’s theorem says any two matrices of 𝒜(R,S) are joined by some sequence of interchanges — i.e. G(R,S) is connected.

Brualdi’s conjecture, and the theorem we prove

Conjecture (Brualdi). G(R,S) is Hamiltonian for all R,S. (In E1, the triangle v0—v1—v2—v0 is such a cycle.)

Definition. A graph is Hamilton-connected if every pair of distinct vertices is joined by a Hamilton path; a bipartite graph is Hamilton-laceable if every opposite-colour pair is (the best possible there, since a Hamilton path alternates colours). A graph is maximally Hamiltonian if it is Hamilton-laceable when bipartite and Hamilton-connected when not.

Theorem 1.1. For every realizable R,S (𝒜(R,S)≠∅), G(R,S) is maximally Hamiltonian; in particular Brualdi’s conjecture holds.

Worked example (E1, non-bipartite). The triangle is non-bipartite, so the claim is Hamilton-connected: e.g. v0→v2→v1 is a Hamilton path from v0 to v1. Verified: Hamilton-connected.

Worked example (E2, bipartite). R=S=(1,1,1) — the six 3×3 permutation matrices; this is CT₃ = K₃,₃, bipartite (even/odd permutations). Here the claim is Hamilton-laceable. Verified: |V|=6, bipartite, Hamilton-laceable. (All six matrices, the colour classes, the fibers/quotient, and a paired-2-DPC cover are in the fold below — it’s the running example for the rest of §2.)

Why the strong form? The proof builds big graphs from small ones (products in §4, fibers over a quotient in §5). A bare Hamilton cycle can’t be steered through those gluings; a Hamilton path between prescribed endpoints can. So maximal Hamiltonicity is what the induction carries — bare Hamiltonicity would not.

The corollary, and the one lemma everything reduces to

A 0/1 matrix with margins R,S is the biadjacency matrix of a bipartite graph with side-degrees R,S, and a 2×2 interchange is a 2-switch — deleting two edges and adding the two “crossed” edges on the same four vertices (the fold below makes this concrete). The realization graph of a degree sequence is the graph whose vertices are all labelled graphs with that degree sequence, two of them joined when one is obtained from the other by a single 2-switch. So G(R,S) is exactly the realization graph of the bipartite degree sequence (R,S):

Corollary. The realization graph of any bipartite degree sequence (equivalently, by Arikati–Peled, any split-graph degree sequence) is maximally Hamiltonian. The arbitrary-degree-sequence version stays open.

▸ See it fully worked out

A (0,1)-matrix is a bipartite graph: put the rows on one side, columns on the other, and an edge rᵢ—cⱼ for each 1 in cell (i,j). Take v0 and v1 of E1:

as a bipartite graph, edges: r0—c0, r0—c1, r1—c2

as a bipartite graph, edges: r0—c0, r0—c2, r1—c1

The interchange v0→v1 removes r0—c1, r1—c2 and adds r0—c2, r1—c1 — i.e. two edges swap their column-endpoints. That is exactly a 2-switch of the bipartite graph. So the interchange graph G(R,S) is the realization graph (2-switch graph) of the bipartite degree sequence (R,S) — which is why the theorem also settles maximal Hamiltonicity of those realization graphs.

Lemma 1.2 (Block Lemma). Every balanced bipartite interchange graph is paired 2-disjoint-path- coverable (§2.4). Proved in §7; used as a black box until then.

§2. Preliminaries

§2.1 Lines, fibers, and quotients

Definition. A line is a row or column. Fix a line L. For each pattern p of L, the fiber F_p is the matrices with L=p; inside it L is frozen, so (deleting L) a fiber is the interchange graph of a smaller class. The quotient Q_L has the patterns of L as vertices, p,q adjacent when one interchange (touching L) carries F_p to F_q.

Worked example (E2, CT₃). Freeze row 0: its single 1 sits in column 0, 1, or 2 — three patterns → three fibers, each (deleting row 0) a K₂; the quotient is K₃. So K₃,₃ is three K₂ fibers over a K₃ quotient. Verified: see the E2 fold under §1 for the three fibers and the quotient, computed from the six matrices. (The reassembly of these pieces into all 9 edges of K₃,₃ is walked in TUTORIAL_QA_pending.md, Q1–Q2.)

§2.2 Invariant positions

Definition. A cell is invariant if constant across the whole class. Two sources: an inactive line (sum 0 or maximal) is all-constant and deletes harmlessly (same graph, smaller matrix; a line that isn’t inactive is active); the remaining invariant cells force a Cartesian factorisation into disjoint blocks. An active class is prime (indecomposable) iff it is invariant-free (Brualdi–Manber). Prime ≠ connected: C₄=K₂□K₂ is connected yet decomposable.

Worked example (E3) — inactive row, then a leftover product. R=(4,3,3,1,1), S=(4,4,2,2). Row 0 has sum 4 = #columns, so it is all-1s in every matrix → inactive; delete it. In the remaining 4×4, every line is active, yet forced corners (top-left all-1, bottom-right all-0) leave two independent 2×2 permutation blocks → G = K₂ □ K₂ = C₄. Verified: |V|=4, 12 invariant cells, two product blocks.

§2.3 Bipartiteness

Lemma 2.1. An interchange graph is bipartite iff triangle-free — special to interchange graphs (in general, triangle-free is weaker; here any odd cycle already forces a triangle).

Worked examples. E2 K₃,₃ is triangle-free → bipartite. E1 (1 triangle) and E4 (8 triangles) are non-bipartite. Verified: triangle counts 0 / 1 / 8 respectively. (This is the engine of the §5 “buffer”: a non-bipartite class is guaranteed a triangle — see TUTORIAL_QA_pending.md Q4.)

§2.4 Disjoint path covers

Definition. For two disjoint pairs (a₁,b₁),(a₂,b₂), a paired 2-disjoint path cover is disjoint paths P₁:a₁→b₁, P₂:a₂→b₂ together covering all vertices. In a bipartite graph a demand is balanced if each pair is opposite-coloured; the graph is paired 2-DPC if every balanced demand has a cover. Strictly stronger than laceability (the one-pair case); it is what survives products.

Worked example (E2, K₃,₃). A balanced demand and an explicit disjoint covering pair are shown in the E2 fold under §1. Verified: K₃,₃ is paired 2-DPC — all 72 balanced demands have a cover.

Worked example (why balanced is required, E3 =C₄). For C₄ the balanced demands are all coverable, but the unbalanced (same-coloured pairs) version fails. Verified: C₄ covers balanced 8/8 but unbalanced only 8/12.

§2.5 Complete transposition graphs

Definition. CT_a = the Cayley graph of S_a with all transpositions = G(R,S) for all-ones a×a margins, so |V(CT_a)|=a!; bipartite, and balanced for a≥2. Complementary margins (a−1,…,a−1) give the co-permutation class, isomorphic to CT_a via A↦J−A.

Worked examples. CT₂=K₂; CT₃=K₃,₃ (E2).

Proposition 2.2. Every bipartite interchange graph is a Cartesian product of complete transposition graphs (and co-permutation copies); nontrivially, it is balanced and of even order. This is the bridge handing the whole bipartite case to the Block Lemma (§7) as a CT-product.

Worked example (E3 =C₄). C₄ = K₂ □ K₂ = CT₂ □ CT₂ — a product of two complete transposition graphs, as Proposition 2.2 predicts; CT₃=K₃,₃ (E2) is the prime one-factor case. Verified: E3 is bipartite with two CT₂ blocks; E2 is bipartite and prime.

§3. The reduction

The proof is an induction on |V(G)|, with the Block Lemma (§7) as a standing black box. Given a class G, run this decision tree; every branch either finishes G outright or hands it to strictly smaller classes (so the induction hypothesis applies) or to the Block Lemma.

  1. Is G bipartite? (Lemma 2.1 — equivalently, triangle-free.) If yes, Proposition 2.2 makes it a product of complete-transposition blocks, the Block Lemma (§7) makes it paired 2-DPC, hence Hamilton-laceable. Done — no induction used.
  2. Otherwise delete inactive lines to the active core (§2.2). Does the core have an invariant cell? If yes, it is a nontrivial Cartesian product A □ B of strictly smaller classes (at least one non-bipartite, since a product is bipartite only if both factors are); §4 lifts maximal Hamiltonicity from the factors.
  3. Otherwise the core is prime (invariant-free). Are there ≤ 2 active rows, or ≤ 2 active columns? If yes, G is a Johnson graph J(f,k) — §6 (Alspach).
  4. Otherwise there are ≥ 3 active rows and ≥ 3 active columns. If |V|≤6 it is a small base class (§6); if |V|>6 it is the genuinely many-line pivot case (§5).

Worked example — classify the running examples. Sorting each class by the tree: E1 → Johnson, E2 → CORE, E5 → product, the base core → base, E4 → pivot. The full decision facts (bipartite? invariant cells? dimensions? |V|?) for each are in the fold.

§4. Cartesian products

When the active class has an invariant cell it is a product G = A □ B of two strictly smaller interchange graphs (§2.2), each maximally Hamiltonian by induction; since G is non-bipartite, at least one factor is. Goal: show G is Hamilton-connected.

The picture (layers and rungs). View G as |V(B)| copies of A (“layers”), one per vertex of B; two layers over B-adjacent vertices are joined by the identity matching on the A-coordinate (“rungs”). A Hamilton path follows a spanning walk of B, crossing each visited layer by a path of A and stepping between layers along rungs. Three cases:

  1. Both factors non-bipartite — both Hamilton-connected, no parity device needed: thread a Hamilton path of one factor, cross each copy of the other between its splice points. (K_4 □ K_3 is such a class.)
  2. One factor bipartite — the substantive case, Proposition 4.1.
  3. Degenerate A = K_2 — too small for the device; a direct comb.

Proposition 4.1. If A is balanced bipartite and paired 2-disjoint-path-coverable with |V(A)|≥4, and B is non-bipartite and Hamilton-connected, then A □ B is Hamilton-connected.

The obstruction: A bipartite means crossing a layer flips its colour, so threading single layers forces a fixed relation between the endpoint colours that need not hold. The fix: double one layer — cover it by a paired 2-cover of A (two disjoint paths) instead of one path, absorbing the parity. A controlled spanning walk of B (of the right parity, repeating no vertex more than twice) picks which layer to double; Lemma 4.2 guarantees that layer’s four terminals are distinct and form two opposite-coloured pairs (this is where |V(A)|≥4 is used); the boundaries are assigned globally and the layer pieces spliced.

Worked example (the substantive case): C_4 □ K_3. A = C_4 (bipartite, |V|=4, the E3 graph), B = K_3 (non-bipartite, E1): three layers of C_4 over K_3, every two layers fully runged. An actual lifted Hamilton path — with the doubled layer visible as the one the path enters twice — is in the fold. Verified: Hamilton-connected (all 66 pairs).

§5. The pivot construction

The deepest part. Regime: G is active, invariant-free (prime), non-bipartite, with m,n ≥ 3 active rows and columns and |V| > 6; fix endpoints a,b. Plan: pick a buffer line to pivot on — one whose quotient is Hamilton-connected and one of whose fibers is non-bipartite — take a Hamilton path through the quotient, and lift it fiber by fiber into a Hamilton path of G.

Running example E4 = (2,1,1)/(1,1,1,1) (|V|=12): pivoting a sum-1 row gives four triangle fibers over a K_4 quotient — the cleanest instance. For the subtler constrained-fiber machinery we also use E7 = (2,2,1)/(2,1,1,1), whose pivot gives three bipartite K_2 fibers plus one non-bipartite buffer.

Setup (5.1–5.3)

  • Lemma 5.1 (canonical fibers). Fixing the pivot line and deleting it, each fiber — after its own inactive-line deletion and invariant split — is a product of strictly smaller interchange graphs, so the induction hypothesis applies. (E4: each fiber is the residual (2,1)/(1,1,1) class = a triangle.)
  • Lemma 5.2 (pivot-freedom). In a prime block every cell is active (the active row–column graph is K_{m,n}) — there is always room to pivot.
  • Lemma 5.3 (#2-nail). If a pair were “trapped” (every separating line has ≤3 patterns) then |V|≤6; so in the non-base case some separating line has ≥4 patterns. (E4: the pivot row has 4 patterns.)

The quotient is a Hamilton-connected matroid graph (5.4–5.5)

  • Lemma 5.4 / 5.4a (matroid quotient). The realizable patterns of a line are the bases of a matroid, and every basis exchange is realized by an actual inter-fiber interchange — so Q_L is the matroid’s basis-exchange graph (the 1-skeleton of the base polytope).
  • Corollary 5.5 (Case A). A non-bipartite matroid basis-exchange graph is Hamilton-connected (Naddef–Pulleyblank: a (0,1)-polytope skeleton is a hypercube or Hamilton-connected; non-bipartite rules out the hypercube). (E4: Q_L = K_4.)

The interface: room to splice (5.6, 5.7, 5.7c, 5.7b)

Crossing from one fiber to the next needs choices of entry/exit vertices.

  • Lemma 5.7 (interface ≥ 2). A non-bipartite fiber on a used edge offers ≥ 2 crossing vertices.
  • Lemma 5.7c (reachable-terminal richness). From a constrained fiber C into a non-bipartite fiber E, each colour class of C reaches ≥ 2 distinct vertices of E. The clean proof rests on the column-count identity b(X) − a(X) = D, D = s_j − s_i + 1, forcing D ∈ {0} ∪ {≥2} (D=1 and D<0 impossible); D≥2 gives b≥2 at once, D=0 splits into a K_2 case and a clique case. This lets a constrained fiber, whose exit colour is forced, still hand the buffer an entry vertex distinct from its pinned exit.

§7. The Block Lemma

By Proposition 2.2 the Block Lemma is:

Theorem 7.1. Every Cartesian product of complete transposition graphs (order ≥ 4) is paired 2-disjoint-path-coverable.

The engine (Coleman et al. 2025). A weld of equal-order graphs is their disjoint union plus a perfect matching between every pair; a transposition-like graph of rank r is a weld of ≥ r rank-(r−1) ones (rank 1 = any Hamilton-connected/laceable graph). Each CT_a is rank a (its a cosets are copies of CT_{a−1}).

  • Theorem 7.2 (Coleman). A bipartite transposition-like graph of rank n, whose rank-1 leaves are single vertices or even-order, has a paired-(n−1)-disjoint path cover.
  • Theorem 7.3 (Coleman, down-closure). Paired-k-DPC ⇒ paired-ℓ-DPC for every ℓ ≤ k.

A single block. For a≥3, CT_a is rank a with single-vertex leaves → paired-(a−1)-DPC (7.2) → paired-2-DPC (7.3).

Arbitrary products (double induction on the number of factors). All-CT_2 products are hypercubes, paired-2-DPC by Jo–Park–Chwa. Otherwise write G = CT_a □ B (a≥3); by the outer induction B is even-order Hamilton-laceable, so CT_a □ B is a rank-a transposition-like graph with leaf B, and 7.2 + 7.3 give paired-2-DPC. The two inductions act on different parameters and don’t interact.

Worked example. K_{3,3}=CT_3 is paired-2-DPC (single block); C_4=CT_2□CT_2 (a product / hypercube) is paired-2-DPC; and the weld (CT_3 = 3 welded copies of K_2) is the §2.1 fibers/quotient again. The fold has the counts and the structure.

Single block: CT_3 = K_{3,3}. Paired-2-DPC — all balanced demands covered: 72/72. (Theorem 7.2 + down-closure 7.3.)

The weld that drives the induction: freezing the last symbol splits CT_3 into 3 cosets, each a copy of CT_2 = K_2, matched pairwise — exactly the 3 fibers over the K_3 quotient from §2.1. In general CT_a is a weld of a copies of CT_{a-1}, and CT_a □ B a weld of a copies of CT_{a-1} □ B.

A product: C_4 = CT_2 □ CT_2 (the hypercube Q_2, = E3). Paired-2-DPC — balanced demands covered: 8/8. Products of CT_2’s (hypercubes) are the base of the outer induction (Jo–Park–Chwa); larger products fold in one CT_a at a time with B as an even Hamilton-laceable leaf. The general statement is Coleman et al. 2025 (Theorem 7.2) — the one deep external input, which a referee must confirm applies at every rank.

This is the one place the proof leans on a recent external theorem (Coleman 2025) rather than rebuilding from smaller — the most load-bearing citation, and the first thing a referee should scrutinise.

§8. What’s proved, and what a referee must still check

Combining §§3–6 (the reduction) with §7 (the Block Lemma) proves Theorem 1.1: every interchange graph is maximally Hamiltonian, settling Brualdi’s conjecture.

The honest boundary. The worked examples here are verified on their instances, and each load-bearing lemma is checked on all small classes — but that is a finite range. The leap to the infinite family rests on (a) the induction and our general lemma proofs, and (b) the cited theorems at all scales (Coleman 2025 first, then Naddef–Pulleyblank, Araki, Alspach, Jo–Park–Chwa, Brualdi/Brualdi–Manber).
